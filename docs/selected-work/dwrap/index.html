Who Hosts the World's Refugees?

The past year has seen a spike in anti-immigrant rhetoric: In the West, governments have doubled down on caps limiting the number of hosted refugees. The United States’ cap of hosted refugees fell 94% in 2026, to a historic low of 7500. High-income European countries have tightened restrictions, both restricting arrivals and speeding up deportations, in reaction to what some migration critics have called a “refugee crisis.”

However, the majority of the world’s displaced has never been hosted by Global North countries. In the seven decades since the passage of the 1951 Refugee Convention—the modern asylum framework—the vast majority of the world’s refugees have been hosted by developing countries.

The study of migration law has historically focused on that of Global North countries. However, if the vast majority of refugees are received by Global South countries, the lack of data on these countries’ laws represents a critical gap in understanding global migratory flows.

Source: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder. Shading uses a log scale to account for extreme right-skew. Income group labels: World Bank. Flows shown for most recent available year.

The Developing World Refugee and Asylum Policy (DWRAP) Dataset aims to change this. Published in 2022, DWRAP tracks asylum law across 205 countries since the passage of the 1951 Refugee Convention, and constitutes the largest to-date codification on how asylum policies have evolved over time in the Global South.

The Developing World Refugee and Asylum Policy (DWRAP) Dataset aims to change this. Published in 2022, DWRAP tracks asylum law across 205 countries since the passage of the 1951 Refugee Convention, and constitutes the largest to-date codification on how asylum policies have evolved over time in the Global South.

While Global South countries tend to have less liberal asylum policies than Global North countries—in areas like employment rights and political participation—they have, on average, liberalized faster than their Global North counterparts.

Latin America, for example, significantly expanded its legal protections for refugees in the early twenty-first century, while also hosting more refugees due to recent displacement crises. This stands in contrast to some European countries, who—while technically boasting liberal asylum law—has de facto limited asylum seekers’ access to protection and rights.
