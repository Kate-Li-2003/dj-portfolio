Who Hosts the World's Refugees?

Seven decades of asylum law — and why the burden falls where it does

Data: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder; Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies (Blair, Grossman & Weinstein / World Bank, 2024)  ·  Analysis period: 1951–2022

In 2022, Uganda — with a GDP per capita of roughly $800 — was home to more than 1.5 million refugees, the largest population on the African continent. Germany, with an economy more than ninety times larger, hosted fewer. This inversion is not an anomaly. It is the defining pattern of global refugee protection.

Over the seven decades since the 1951 Refugee Convention established the modern framework for asylum, the burden of hosting the world's displaced has migrated steadily toward the countries least equipped to bear it. What began as a mostly European response to a European crisis has become, in practice, an obligation shouldered almost entirely by the Global South.

New data from the World Bank makes that pattern — and its policy implications — quantifiable for the first time at global scale. The Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies (DWRAP), assembled by researchers at Princeton, the University of Pennsylvania, and Harvard, codes 54 dimensions of asylum law across 205 countries from 1951 to 2022. What it reveals is a system under strain in places that receive the least international attention, and a divergence between where refugees go and where the law is most prepared to receive them.

Part I

The Geography of Exile

Displacement does not travel far. The vast majority of the world's refugees cross into a neighboring country — often one that shares the same economic precarity, the same political instability, or the same climate vulnerability as the country they fled. Afghanistan's neighbors host Afghans. South Sudan's neighbors host South Sudanese. Syria's neighbors — Turkey, Lebanon, Jordan — absorbed millions after 2011, straining systems that were never designed for that scale.

The chart below maps those flows. Each ribbon represents people moving from a region of origin to a host-country income group. The width of each band is proportional to the number of displaced persons. The story it tells is simple: the flows run overwhelmingly toward countries the World Bank classifies as low- or middle-income.

Source: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder via the refugees R package. Ribbon width = total refugees + asylum-seekers. Income classifications: World Bank. Flows < 1,000 persons suppressed for legibility.

The pattern holds across decades and across crises. During the Cold War, refugees from Southeast Asia and Central America flowed into neighboring low-income states. During the 1990s, the Rwandan genocide and the Balkan wars generated massive regional displacement. After 2011, the Syrian civil war produced the largest single-country refugee crisis since World War II — and the countries that absorbed the most were, by every measure, among the least wealthy.

"In 2017, roughly 70% of all asylum-seekers originated from DWRAP-covered countries — and 35% sought refuge in them." — Blair, Grossman & Weinstein, International Organization, 2022
Part II

Where the Weight Lands

Aggregate numbers obscure geography. The top 40 bilateral flows — the largest individual country-to-country movements — are overlaid as arcs. Their width is proportional to the number of people.

Source: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder. Shading uses a log scale to account for extreme right-skew. Income group labels: World Bank. Flows shown for most recent available year.

A handful of countries carry a disproportionate share. Uganda, with one of the most open land-border policies in the world, consistently ranks among the top five hosts globally — not just in absolute numbers but per capita. Lebanon, at its peak, hosted more refugees relative to its population than any country on earth: roughly one in four residents was a Syrian or Palestinian displaced person. Jordan has hosted Palestinian refugees continuously since 1948.

What these countries share, beyond proximity to crisis zones, is limited capacity. Foreign aid for refugee hosting is concentrated in a handful of UNHCR operations. Resettlement — the process by which a refugee in a host country is moved to a third country, usually a wealthy one — reached only 114,000 people in 2022, a fraction of the more than 100 million displaced worldwide. The gap between the scale of need and the architecture of response is not accidental. It is structural.

Part III

Seven Decades of Concentration

The asymmetry between the Global North and Global South in refugee hosting is not new — but it has grown. The line chart below tracks the share of the world's total displaced population hosted by developing countries from 1951 to 2022. Each year is a data point; the overall trend is unmistakable.

Source: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder via the refugees R package. "Developing" = World Bank non-high-income classification. Series begins with first year of reliable bilateral UNHCR data.

Several inflection points are visible in the trend. The early 1990s saw a sharp rise corresponding to simultaneous crises in the former Yugoslavia, the Great Lakes region of Africa, and Afghanistan. A brief plateau followed, then the share climbed again after 2011 — driven not only by the Syrian crisis but by the simultaneous hardening of asylum access in Europe and North America. When wealthy countries restrict entry, the share absorbed by developing nations rises not because the South becomes more welcoming, but because the alternative closes.

The countries that receive the most refugees are rarely the ones writing op-eds about a refugee crisis.
Part IV

The Policy Paradox

Here is where the story becomes counterintuitive. If you expected that the countries bearing the greatest hosting burden would also have the most restrictive asylum laws — a political response to pressure — the data suggest otherwise. In aggregate, the Global South has been liberalizing its asylum frameworks at a faster rate than the Global North.

DWRAP scores each country-year on a policy liberality index ranging from 0 to 1, constructed from 54 individual legal provisions: the right to non-refoulement, access to courts, work authorization, freedom of movement, access to education and health care, and more. The chart below shows individual country trajectories in light gray, with the group mean for Global North and Global South countries drawn in bold.

Source: DWRAP (Blair, Grossman & Weinstein / World Bank, 2024). "Global North" = World Bank High-Income countries. Index constructed by inverse covariance-weighting across 54 policy provisions, scaled 0–1. The horizontal reference line marks the international legal standard derived from the 1951 Convention and its 1967 Protocol.

The DWRAP index is a measure of what the law says, not what governments do. A country can pass a progressive refugee law and enforce it inconsistently, or not at all. The researchers behind the dataset are explicit about this: DWRAP captures the de jure environment, not the de facto one. That caveat matters. But it does not undermine the finding.

The liberalization trend in Global South legal frameworks corresponds, roughly, to the diffusion of international norms. The 1969 OAU Convention on Refugee Problems in Africa expanded the 1951 Convention's definition of a refugee to include people fleeing generalized violence — a standard more permissive than the original text. The 1984 Cartagena Declaration did the same in Latin America. Regional frameworks built on the global baseline in ways the Global North, largely satisfied with the 1951 text, did not replicate.

What the DWRAP data surface is the distance between those frameworks and the international legal standard — and how that distance has narrowed unevenly. Many Global South countries are still below the threshold that full compliance with the 1951 Convention would imply. But the trajectory is upward, and in some dimensions — particularly access to education and freedom of movement in parts of sub-Saharan Africa — the Global South has moved further than commonly assumed.

Part V

Policy as Invitation

The trend lines above raise a question that aggregate data cannot answer: why does a government liberalize its asylum law? One explanation rooted in the international relations literature is the ethnic kin hypothesis — the idea that states extend more generous legal frameworks when they anticipate receiving displaced co-ethnics fleeing persecution or conflict in a neighboring country. The host state, in other words, shapes its law to match the population it expects to arrive.

Two case studies from the DWRAP dataset illustrate the dynamic. In both, a country's policy liberality index — tracking the actual text of domestic law — moves in tandem with a surge in arrivals from a neighboring state whose ethnic composition maps onto the host country's own.

Case Study I

Armenia & Azerbaijan, 1991–2002

When the Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, the unresolved territorial dispute over Nagorno-Karabakh — an Armenian-majority enclave inside Azerbaijan — erupted into full-scale war. By the time a ceasefire was reached in 1994, an estimated one million ethnic Armenians had been expelled from Azerbaijan, and roughly 800,000 ethnic Azerbaijanis had fled Armenia. The displacement was mutual, rapid, and ethnically sorted.

Armenia, as the ethnic homeland, had strong incentives to construct a legal framework capable of receiving its expelled co-ethnics. The pattern is imperfect — Armenia was itself an impoverished, newly independent state absorbing displacement while managing its own economic collapse. But the direction of the policy signal is consistent with the ethnic kin logic: law was shaped, at least in part, by the character of those expected to arrive.

Case Study II

Uganda & Rwanda, 1990–2002

Uganda's relationship with Rwandan displacement is long and layered. Rwandan Tutsi began fleeing into Uganda as early as 1959, during the violence that preceded Rwandan independence. By the 1980s, a generation of Rwandan refugees had grown up in Uganda — many of them veterans of Uganda's own civil conflict, who formed the core of the Rwandan Patriotic Front (RPF) that invaded Rwanda in 1990.

When the 1994 genocide killed an estimated 800,000 people in 100 days, Uganda was not an uninformed bystander. Its historical and ethnic ties to Rwandan Tutsis were deep, and its government had tacitly supported the RPF's operations. The policy response came four years later: Uganda's 1998 Refugees Act, widely cited as one of sub-Saharan Africa's most progressive refugee frameworks, formalized protections that had often existed informally for decades.

The chart below maps Uganda's policy index against the recorded flow of Rwandan refugees and asylum-seekers. The trajectory of both — displacement preceding, then prompting, legal consolidation — is a textbook instance of policy following population.

Source: DWRAP (Blair, Grossman & Weinstein / World Bank, 2024); UNHCR via refugees R package.

These two cases do not prove the ethnic kin hypothesis — no two cases can. But they illustrate a mechanism that aggregate cross-national analysis can obscure: the decision to liberalize asylum law is often not an abstract humanitarian commitment, but a response to a specific anticipated population, arriving from a specific place, for reasons that are well understood by the receiving government long before the first crossing.

That specificity is both the strength and the limit of the DWRAP approach. By coding de jure policy — what the law says — the dataset captures the signal of deliberate legislative choice. What it cannot capture is whether that choice was ever operationalized, or whether it quietly expired when the political moment passed.

Conclusion

A System Built on the Wrong Premise

The 1951 Refugee Convention was designed in the aftermath of a European catastrophe, by countries that expected to be the primary source — and primary solution — to mass displacement. That premise has not held. The geography of crisis has shifted southward, and with it the geography of hosting. The legal architecture, however, remains anchored to a world that no longer exists.

What the DWRAP data suggest is that the countries doing the most — absorbing the largest populations, passing the most expansive legal frameworks — are also the ones with the fewest resources to make those frameworks real. The gap between law on paper and protection in practice is widest precisely where the need is greatest.

That gap is not inevitable. It is a policy choice, made repeatedly, by governments and international institutions that have the capacity to close it and have not. The numbers are now available to describe it precisely. What remains is the harder work of deciding what to do.

Methodology & Data Notes

DWRAP: The Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies codes 54 provisions of national asylum and refugee law across 205 countries, 1951–2022, on ordinal 0–3 scales. Provisions are aggregated using inverse covariance-weighting (Anderson 2008) into 14 policy strands, 5 policy fields, and an overall policy index scaled 0–1. Laws are identified primarily through UNHCR submissions to the UN Universal Periodic Review, supplemented by UNHCR Refworld, ILO NATLEX, and the Law Library of Congress. The dataset codes de jure policies — legal texts — not enforcement or de facto outcomes. Citation: World Bank; with Christopher W. Blair, Guy Grossman and Jeremy M. Weinstein. 2024. "Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies." World Bank Development Data Hub. Data link ↗

UNHCR data: Refugee and asylum-seeker counts are sourced from the UNHCR Population Statistics database via the refugees R package (UNHCR, 2023). Bilateral origin-to-asylum flows for years prior to 1975 are sparse; trend analysis begins from 1960.

Income classifications: Country income groups (High Income / Upper Middle / Lower Middle / Low) are from the World Bank Development Indicators via the wbstats R package. "Global North" is operationalized as World Bank High-Income countries; "Global South" as all others. An OECD-membership alternative is available in the underlying analysis files.

Analysis files: All underlying R Markdown code is available in the project repository.
