Who Hosts the World's Refugees?

Seven decades of asylum law — and why the burden falls where it does

Data: UNHCR Refugee Data Finder; Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies  ·  Analysis period: 1951–2022

In 2022, Uganda — with a GDP per capita of roughly $800 — was home to more than 1.5 million refugees, the largest population on the African continent. Germany, with an economy more than ninety times larger, hosted fewer. This trend has largely characterized global refugee patterns for the past century.

Over the seven decades since the 1951 Refugee Convention established the modern framework for asylum, the burden of hosting the world's displaced has migrated steadily toward the countries least equipped to bear it. New data codifying trends in refugee and asylum law, called the Developing World Refugee and Asylum Policy Dataset, tracks this by codifying 54 dimensions of asylum law across 205 countries from 1951 to 2022. It shows a steady divergence between where refugees go, and where the law is most prepared to receive them.

Part I

Displacement Patterns

Refugees fleeing crisis are typically unable to travel far distances. The vast majority of the world's refugees cross into a neighboring country — often ones that share the same political precarity as the ones they fled.

The chart below illustrates patterns of refugee migration in 2022, where the vast majority of refugee flows run toward countries the World Bank classifies as low- or middle-income.

The pattern has held over time; during the Cold War, refugees from Southeast Asia and Central America flowed into neighboring low-income states. During the 1990s, the Rwandan genocide and the Balkan wars generated massive regional displacement. After 2011, the Syrian civil war produced the largest single-country refugee crisis since World War II. Countries which recieved these refugees were, by every measure, among the least wealthy.

A handful of countries feel disproportionate burden; for example, Uganda, which has one of the most open land-border policies in the world, consistently ranks among the top five hosts globally — not just in absolute numbers but per capita. Similarly, Lebanon, at its peak, hosted more refugees relative to its population than any country on earth: roughly one in four residents was a Syrian or Palestinian displaced person. Jordan has hosted Palestinian refugees continuously since 1948.

Part IV

Liberality Over Time

Although Global South countries tend to have less liberal asylum and refugee policy than that of Global North countries, they have - on aggregate - liberalized their asylum policy faster than Global North countries.

Source: DWRAP (Blair, Grossman & Weinstein / World Bank, 2024). "Global North" = World Bank High-Income countries. Index constructed by inverse covariance-weighting across 54 policy provisions, scaled 0–1. The horizontal reference line marks the international legal standard derived from the 1951 Convention and its 1967 Protocol.

These liberalization trends in legal frameworks correspond, roughly, to international norms. For instance, the 1969 OAU Convention on Refugee Problems in Africa expanded the 1951 Convention's definition of a refugee to include people fleeing generalized violence — a standard more permissive than the original text. The 1984 Cartagena Declaration did the same in Latin America. Regional frameworks built on the 1951 UNHCR Refugee Convention in ways the Global North haven't replicated.

Part V

Explaining Policy Liberalization

While data on aggregate has shown that Global South countries have become more accomodating to refugees over time, the exact motivations for such trends aren't obvious. However, DWRAP's dataset can also instantiate current hypotheses in the international relations literature: that states extend more generous legal frameworks when their leadership anticipates ethnic kin, who may be fleeing persecution or conflict in a neighboring country.

We consider trends in the data for two countries to illustrate this dynamic. DWRAP quantifies how countries whose leadership anticipated outflows of ethnic kin, like Armenia and Uganda, liberalized their policies shortly after the breakout of war or persecution.

When the Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, the unresolved territorial dispute over Nagorno-Karabakh — an Armenian-majority enclave inside Azerbaijan — erupted into full-scale war. An estimated one million ethnic Armenians were expelled from Azerbaijan, and roughly 800,000 ethnic Azerbaijanis fled Armenia by the time a ceasefire was reached. Shortly afterward, Armenian asylum policy experienced a broad liberalization, demonstrating the malleability of domestic law to changes in global persecution patterns.

Similarly, Rwandan Tutsi began fleeing into Uganda as early as 1959, during violence that preceded Rwandan independence. When the 1994 genocide killed an estimated 800,000 people in 100 days, the Ugandan government's tacit support for the RPF's operations manifested in a change in Uganda's 1998 Refugees Act, which formalized protections for asylum-seekers.

While these cases do not prove generalizable trends across countries, they suggest the influence of refugee flows on domestic legislation. While media coverage has historially emphasized refugee inflows to liberal democracies, DWRAP data demonstrate that they are often recieved by neighboring countries - some of whom may liberalize policy specifically to recieve them.

Methodology & Data

DWRAP: The Dataset of World Refugee and Asylum Policies codes 54 provisions of national asylum and refugee law across 205 countries, 1951–2022, on ordinal 0–3 scales. Provisions are aggregated using inverse covariance-weighting (Anderson 2008) into 14 policy strands, 5 policy fields, and an overall policy index scaled 0–1. Laws are identified primarily through UNHCR submissions to the UN Universal Periodic Review, supplemented by UNHCR Refworld, ILO NATLEX, and the Law Library of Congress. The dataset codes de jure policies — legal texts — not enforcement or de facto outcomes.
